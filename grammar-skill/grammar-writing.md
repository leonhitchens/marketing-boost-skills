# Grammar Writing Skill

This skill is built on the grammar standards established by Ruskin Consulting in their internal Training Module 1. The rules apply to any written content — not just marketing or agency work. Use them when writing or reviewing anything that needs to be clear, correct, and professional.

---

## Rule 1: Sentence Structure

Every sentence needs a subject and a predicate.

- **Subject:** the main noun the sentence is about
- **Predicate:** a clause with a verb that gives information about the subject

**Parallel structure:** When listing items or actions, keep the same grammatical form across the list.
- Correct: "Joey likes to eat chips and watch shows."
- Correct: "The store accepts cash, credit cards, and online payments."

**No double negatives:** Two negatives make a positive and change the meaning.
- Incorrect: "You can't prove nothing." (means you CAN prove something)
- Correct: "You can't prove anything."

**Complete comparisons:** Always include at least two things being compared.
- Correct: "Jill's salary is higher than Jack's."
- Correct: "We have more clicks this week than we had last week."

---

## Rule 2: Punctuation

### Periods
End every sentence with a period.

### Colons
Use colons to introduce a list or emphasize a title.
- "I need the following items: butter, sugar, eggs, and flour."
- "Avatar: The Way of the Water"

### Semicolons
Use semicolons to connect two complete, related sentences.
- "I have a test tomorrow; I can't go out tonight."

### Contractions
Use contractions to shorten words followed by "have", "not", or "is/are".
- "You shouldn't go without an umbrella when it's raining."
- "You could've made that bet."

### Commas
Use commas in these situations:

| Use | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Phrases | Separate an introductory phrase from the main clause | "In the beginning, there was light." |
| Lists | Separate items in a list | "She bought apples, oranges, and grapes." |
| Parenthetical | Add extra info that could be removed | "Soccer, which is good for your health, is the most popular sport in the world." |
| Numbers | Space out large numbers | "1,000,000" |
| Dates | Between the day and year | "January 12, 2023" |

### Capitalization

| Case | Rule | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Starting | First word of every sentence | "We manage Google Ads accounts." |
| Names | People, locations, holidays, institutions | "Kobe Bryant", "New Year's Eve", "Federal Bureau of Investigation" |
| Titles | First letter of important words in job titles, books, articles | "Chief Executive Officer", "Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets" |

### Numbers

| Type | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Start of sentence | Write out the word | "Two million years ago..." |
| Less than 10 | Write out the word | "one, two, three" |
| Fractions | Use numerals | "1/2 cup of sugar" |
| Decimals | Use numerals | "13.7 billion years old" |
| Measurements | Use numerals | "3 feet, 12 meters, 36 years" |

## Rule 3: Nouns

### A vs. An
Use "an" before words that start with a vowel sound. Use "a" before words that start with a consonant sound.
- "Bring a coat." vs. "Bring an umbrella."
- "It was a 10% increase." vs. "It was an 11% increase." (eleven starts with a vowel sound)

### Singular vs. Plural
Add "s" to make a noun plural. If the word ends in "s", add "es". Change "is" to "are" when switching to plural.
- "My car is fast." vs. "Cars are fast."
- "We got 1 click." vs. "We got 10 clicks."

### Possessive
Add "'s" when a noun has possession of something.
- "Tim has that account." becomes "This is Tim's account."
- "Alex has a dog named Max." becomes "Max is Alex's dog."

## Rule 4: Verb Tenses

Use the correct tense, especially in monthly reports. Most reporting is written in past tense.

| Tense | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | verb | "I call", "It increases" |
| Past | verb + d/ed | "I called", "It increased" |
| Future | will/going to + verb | "I will call", "It is going to increase" |
| Present Perfect | has/have + verb + d/ed | "I have called", "It has increased" |

**Monthly reports:** Use past tense for anything that already happened during the reporting period. Use present tense for observations about the current state of the account.

## Rule 5: Adjectives and Adverbs

**Adjectives** describe nouns.
**Adverbs** describe verbs. Most adverbs are formed by adding "-ly" to an adjective.

| Noun/Verb | Adjective | Adverb |
|---|---|---|
| I work. | I am efficient. | I work efficiently. |
| You run. | You are quick. | You run quickly. |
| He writes. | He is creative. | He writes creatively. |

### Order of Adjectives
When listing multiple adjectives, use this order:

1. Quantity
2. Opinion
3. Age
4. Size
5. Color
6. Shape

Examples:
- "I have three, delicious, big, red apples."
- "Mrs. Smith owns eight, cute, small, brown, fluffy cats."
- "The new, 2x2, red images for the display campaign were good."

## Rule 6: Commonly Confused Words

| Confused Pair | Rule | Correct Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **To / Two / Too** | "To" = direction, "Two" = number, "Too" = also/excessive | "John will go to Paris." / "I have two eyes." / "It is too expensive." |
| **Their / They're / There** | "Their" = possessive, "They're" = they are, "There" = location | "This is their house." / "They're willing to trade." / "I want to go there." |
| **Your / You're** | "Your" = possessive, "You're" = you are | "Your money has increased." / "You're likely to succeed." |
| **Who's / Whose** | "Who's" = who is, "Whose" = possessive | "Who's going to wash the dishes?" / "Whose dishes were left in the sink?" |
| **It's / Its** | "It's" = it is, "Its" = possessive | "It's fun to read books." / "The dog wagged its tail." |
| **Than / Then** | "Than" = comparison, "Then" = time | "Anna's grades are higher than Elsa's." / "She got her nails done, then had her hair cut." |
| **Effect / Affect** | "Effect" = noun (result), "Affect" = verb (to influence) | "Pollution has a bad effect on the planet." / "Higher temperatures adversely affect the environment." |
| **Except / Accept** | "Except" = excluding, "Accept" = to receive or agree | "I like all colors except pink." / "Soup kitchens accept donations." |
| **Cause / Because** | "Cause" = to make happen, "Because" = explains why | "Earthquakes cause tsunamis." / "Classes are canceled because of the earthquake." |
| **Fewer / Lesser** | "Fewer" = countable things, "Lesser" = degree or extent | "We have fewer conversions this month than last." / "I agree but to a lesser extent." |

## Rule 7: Writing Style Standards

### No Em Dashes
Never use em dashes (--) in any written content. Replace them with a comma, period, colon, or rewrite the sentence.
- Incorrect: "The campaign performed well -- especially in Q4."
- Correct: "The campaign performed well, especially in Q4."
- Incorrect: "We have three options -- call, email, or meet."
- Correct: "We have three options: call, email, or meet."

### Avoid AI-isms
Do not use words and phrases that sound robotic, over-formal, or like AI-generated filler. These weaken writing and make it sound unnatural.

Words and phrases to avoid:
- "Straightforward"
- "Utilize" (use "use" instead)
- "Leverage" (use "use" or be specific)
- "Delve into"
- "Dive deep"
- "In the realm of"
- "It is worth noting that"
- "It is important to note that"
- "Comprehensive"
- "Robust"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Groundbreaking"
- "Seamless"
- "Game-changer"
- "Synergy"
- "Holistic"
- "Transformative"
- "Elevate"
- "Empower"
- "Unlock"
- "Innovative solution"
- "Tailored to your needs"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "At the end of the day"

Write like a person. Use plain, direct language. If a word or phrase sounds like something a chatbot would say, replace it with something simpler and more specific.

## Quick Checklist for Any Piece of Writing

Before finalizing any written content, verify:

- [ ] Every sentence has a subject and a predicate
- [ ] No double negatives
- [ ] Lists are parallel in structure
- [ ] Comparisons include at least two things
- [ ] Periods end every sentence
- [ ] Commas, colons, and semicolons are used correctly
- [ ] Capitalization is correct (sentences, names, titles)
- [ ] Numbers follow the word/numeral rules
- [ ] "A" vs. "an" is correct
- [ ] Verb tense is consistent and appropriate
- [ ] Adjectives are in the correct order
- [ ] Commonly confused words are used correctly
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No AI-isms or filler language
